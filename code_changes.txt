\section*{Code Changes and Implementation Notes}

This document summarises the main changes made to the codebase during the project's final implementation phase, covering market impact modelling, backtesting correctness, strategy alignment, and the integration of the dynamic uncertainty radius approach.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection*{1. Market Impact Model: Almgren-Chriss Square-Root Model}

The original market impact simulator contained a structural bug: a \textit{liquidity factor} term could go negative when trading volume was high relative to a local window average, effectively making execution prices \textit{better} with market impact than without. This is economically nonsensical --- market impact should always be a cost.

We replaced the ad-hoc formula entirely with the Almgren-Chriss (2005) square-root model, which is widely used in academic and industry settings:

\begin{equation}
    \text{impact} = \eta \cdot \sigma \cdot \sqrt{\frac{V_{\text{trade}}}{V_{\text{ADV}}}}
\end{equation}

where $\eta$ is a scaling constant, $\sigma$ is the intraday price volatility over the trade window, $V_{\text{trade}}$ is the number of shares being traded, and $V_{\text{ADV}}$ is the estimated average daily volume. Because all terms are non-negative, the impact is always non-negative by construction, and execution prices are always worse than mid-market.

The parameter $\eta$ is exposed in \texttt{config.yaml} so it can be tuned without touching the code. For the final results we use $\eta = 10$ with an AUM of \$100M, which produces a clearly visible but not catastrophic market impact consistent with a mid-size institutional trading desk. Ticker-specific fallback ADV values (SPY: 80M shares/day, MSFT: 25M, META: 20M) are used when real-time trade data is unavailable.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection*{2. Slippage Sign-Flip Bug in the Momentum Backtest}

A second bug existed in the momentum backtesting engine. The original code injected slippage directly into the price-return series and then multiplied by the \textit{previous} position (exposure). When the strategy was covering a short position (previous exposure $= -1$) and slippage was negative (a cost), the product became positive --- a spurious profit instead of a cost. This caused the ``with market impact'' curve to sometimes outperform ``without market impact,'' which should never happen.

The fix separates slippage from price returns entirely. Gross P\&L is computed from raw price changes, and slippage is computed as an explicit non-negative dollar cost scaled by the \textit{change} in position (not the previous position). The two are then subtracted:

\[
    \text{net P\&L} = \text{gross P\&L} - \text{slippage cost}
\]

After this fix, the ``with market impact'' curve is guaranteed to lie at or below the ``without market impact'' curve for every strategy and ticker.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection*{3. Momentum Trade Frequency Alignment}

The classical momentum strategy was originally configured to trade every 30 minutes intraday (i.e.\ 13 trades per day), while the RL strategies trade once per day. This asymmetry meant momentum incurred roughly 13 times more slippage than the RL strategies under the same $\eta$, making the comparison unfair rather than informative.

We reconfigured momentum to operate at daily frequency by setting \texttt{trade\_frequency = 390} (the number of minutes in a trading day). A small change to the signal-application logic ensures the position is set once at market open and held for the remainder of the day. This gives all strategies a comparable number of trades per day, so the market impact drag reflects differences in position sizing and turnover rather than raw trading frequency.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection*{4. Date Alignment in Performance Comparison}

The comparison script previously aligned the momentum and RL equity curves using an index offset rather than actual calendar dates. Momentum covers the full backtest window (2022-06-09 to 2022-12-09, 128 trading days), while the RL environment requires a 30-day lookback before it can generate the first observation, so its effective start is around 2022-07-25 (97 trading days). The index-offset approximation caused statistics to be computed over slightly different periods depending on the script, producing inconsistent numbers.

The fix stores the actual trading dates inside each RL backtest result file, and the comparison script uses those stored dates to align all curves on a shared calendar axis. All statistics in the final table are computed over the same 97-day window (2022-07-25 to 2022-12-08) for all strategies.

\subsection*{5. Integration of the Dynamic Uncertainty Radius}

The dynamic radius approach was developed in a separate branch and merged into the main backtesting branch. The key idea is to replace the hand-tuned uncertainty radius $\beta = 10^{-4}$ used by the fixed elliptic robust model (p1N2) with a data-driven value calibrated from observed market impact residuals.

The calibration procedure works as follows. The training data (2021-05-09 to 2022-05-09) is split 80/20 chronologically. All strategies --- vanilla PPO, fixed-$\beta$ p1N2, ball (p1), and dynamic radius --- are trained on the same 80\% window to ensure fair comparison. For the dynamic radius model, the nominal (vanilla) policy is then rolled out on the held-out 20\% calibration window simultaneously in two environments: one with market impact disabled (nominal prices) and one with market impact enabled (realised prices). At each trade, the relative price residual is computed and projected onto the elliptic uncertainty set geometry. The calibrated $\beta$ is set to the 90th percentile of these residual norms:

\[
    \beta_{\text{calib}} = \text{quantile}_{0.9}\bigl(\{\|\mathbf{u}_t\|_{\text{elliptic}}\}_{t \in \mathcal{T}_{\text{calib}}}\}\bigr)
\]

This means the robust model is trained with an uncertainty radius large enough to cover 90\% of the actual market impact perturbations observed on held-out data, rather than an arbitrary constant. The robust model is then retrained on the same 80\% training window using this calibrated $\beta$.

All four strategies share the same test period (2022-06-09 to 2022-12-09) and appear as distinct rows in the final comparison table and plots.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection*{6. Training Pipeline Consolidation}

Previously, each strategy had its own standalone training script. These were consolidated into a single \texttt{train.py} entry point that sequentially trains all four strategies per ticker, runs the backtests, and saves results. The number of training episodes was increased from 10 to 100 for the final results.
